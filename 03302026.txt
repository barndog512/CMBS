OFFICE: 
San Francisco office vacancy fell again in the first quarter as AI firms drive record demand (San Francisco Business Times): https://www.bizjournals.com/sanfrancisco/news/2026/03/25/q1-office-market-cbre-net-absorption-vacancy.html?cx_testId=40&cx_testVariant=cx_41&cx_artPos=3#cxrecs_s the positive momentum in the San Francisco office market continued in Q1 2026:  
* Per CBRE, the vacancy rate for San Francisco office fell another 200bps in Q1, with demand reaching all-time highs 
* Granted, while vacancies continued to decline, they are still elevated with the latest rate at 30.8% 
* Availabilities fell an even higher 250bps, ending Q1 2026 at 33.3%
* Q1 saw the highest quarterly net absorption figure since CBRE began tracking the number, at 1.8mm sf 
* Per CBRE, leasing is largely being driven by A.I. companies, however tenant demand is spilling over into other sectors 
* Tenant requirements in the market increased to 8.2mm sf in Q1, an all-time high 
* It is encouraging to see the pipeline grow despite the healthy leasing activity
* Demand is largely being fueled by tenants with requirements of 100,000-sf or more, of which there are 28 currently
* As we've seen in other markets, getting large blocks of space in the top buildings is becoming difficult, with the vacancy rate for the top office buildings (around 5.2mm sf) at only 12.3% (CBRE considers 10% to 12% to be a healthy vacancy rate)   
* The article notes that much of that vacancy for the top buildings is focused in One Market Plaza (OMPT 2017-1MKT), where vacancies are still around 30% after Google vacated 320,000-sf last year  
* This consistent demand could potentially lead to new supply, as there hasn't been a major office development delivered in San Francisco for several years 
The leasing momentum in San Francisco is encouraging, as is the record high for requirements in the market. That has led to a 200bps decline in vacancies in Q1, although the vacancy rate still hasn't broken below 30.0%. As with most markets, the vacancy rate for the highest quality buildings is significantly lower, at only 12.3%.

Office Vacancy Rate Sees First Significant Decline in Years (Globest): https://www.globest.com/2026/03/27/office-vacancy-rate-sees-first-significant-decline-in-years/?kw=Office%20Vacancy%20Rate%20Sees%20First%20Significant%20Decline%20in%20Years&utm_source=email&utm_medium=enl&utm_campaign=newsroomupdate&utm_content=20260327&utm_term=rem&user_id=ef05666df4f7969cfd3243fe6d1335285dbe89e48b603cecb96f6cc2ef07f55b
* While uneven, Yardi's CommercialCafe noted a 200bps decline in national office vacancies YOY, to 17.6% as of 2/2026
* As with San Francisco, the decline in vacancies is positive, however the overall rate is still elevated 
* Tech heavy markets like Seattle, Austin and San Francisco continue to have the highest vacancy rates in the nation, with all over 24% 
* Other markets retaining vacancy rates near or above 20% include Detroit, the broader Bay Area, San Diego, Dallas, Portland, Denver, and D.C. 
* On the plus side, outperformers include some coastal/Sunbelt markets, with Miami having the lowest vacancy rate at 12.8%, with Manhattan and Tampa Bay close behind 
* Boston and L.A. are in the mid-14% range, with the Twin Cities, Phoenix and Charlotte between 16.8% and 17.8% 
* Yardi attributes the improvement to the sharp decline in new office starts, as well as an increase in demolitions or conversions to residential or mixed use 
* As of February 2026, only 28mm sf of space was under construction nationwide, which is low on a historical basis 
* New construction is elevated in several metros though, including Boston which leads the nation at 3.9mm sf 
* Rounding out the top five in terms of construction in process are Manhattan, Dallas, L.A. and San Diego  
* Despite lower vacancies, average office listing rates were still down 2% YOY, highlighting some continued weakness 
* The priciest markets in terms of rents were Manhattan, San Francisco, Miami and the Bay Area
The fact we are finally seeing a decline in national office vacancy rates is good news, however it is still elevated on a historical basis. The relatively low construction pipeline in the context of increasing conversions/demolitions should also be a near term positive for the sector, although tepid job growth and AI related risks persist.   
  
Manhattan Office Conversions to Double After Record Year (Globest): 
https://www.globest.com/2026/03/20/manhattan-office-conversions-to-double-after-record-year/ this story provides an update on office-to-residential conversions in the NYC metro: 
* Per Cushman & Wakefield, there were around 5mm sf of office-to-residential conversions in Manhattan in 2025, which is the highest annual total in 20 years 
* Despite the elevated number, it pales in comparison to the pipeline which has increased to 9.8mm sf 
* This figure includes nine new projects totaling 2mm sf 
* While earlier conversions were more focused in the Downtown submarket, Midtown now has the highest share of proposed conversions, at 51.6% (from 23.4% pre-pandemic) 
* Downtown is now second, at 38.7% of proposed conversions 
* The article cautions that these figures can change, as two sites previously marked for conversion to multifamily appear to be evolving  
* These are 625 Madison Avenue (COMM 2014-CR14 and CR15) and 623 Fifth Avenue, which are now likely shifting to office redevelopment given increasing demand in the submarket 
* Interestingly, class A properties now make up 51.6% of proposed conversions, from only an average of 5.5% between 2004 and 2019
* The article reviews several conversions that are in progress, including 5 Times Square as well as the old Pfizer HQ at 219 and 234 East 42nd Street, the former of which could include as many as 1,200 multifamily units
It is interesting to see conversion activity move towards Midtown Manhattan after some policy changes, as it has previously been more focused downtown. Conversions are also shifting from more of a B/C quality phenomenon, to now over half of the activity being in class A properties. However, we are starting to see some cases where the numbers for office use are actually more attractive than for residential, in select buildings / improving submarkets.

MULTIFAMILY: 
Rents Show Early Signs of Recovery as Market Stabilizes (Globest): https://www.globest.com/2026/03/25/rents-show-early-signs-of-recovery-as-market-stabilizes/?kw=Rents%20Show%20Early%20Signs%20of%20Recovery%20as%20Market%20Stabilizes&utm_position=1&utm_source=email&utm_medium=enl&utm_campaign=multifamilyalert&utm_content=20260325&utm_term=rem&user_id=ef05666df4f7969cfd3243fe6d1335285dbe89e48b603cecb96f6cc2ef07f55b
* Per its March national rent index, Zumper notes that U.S. multifamily rents are seeing some signs of stabilizing, following almost a year of downward pressure
* Rent declines are decelerating, with one BR and two BR rents down 1.4% and 1.3%, respectively, representing the third consecutive month of decelerating drops 
* After a long period of soft rent growth, more normal seasonal patterns are starting to re-emerge 
* While rents are still trending lower, there are signs of improving spring demand that could push pricing higher as we enter the peak leasing season 
* Median asking rents ticked up in March, up 0.1% and 0.2%, respectively, for one and two BR units - the first monthly gains for both unit types since 5/2025 
* As we've noted before, San Francisco is seeing significant strength in the high end market, with one BR rents increasing 18.4% to an all-time high, ahead of the prior peak back in 6/2019 
* Two BR units in San Francisco were up an even greater 22.6%, surpassing the 9/2025 peak 
* Demand in San Francisco is being driven by tech/AI and the RTO push, with occupancy nearing 97% 
* On the other end, many Florida markets remain soft amid slowing in-migration and heavy supply additions  
* Tampa saw the largest drop with one-BR rents off 12%, with other notable decliners including Miami (-3.9%) and Orlando (-3.3%)   
* Other markets seeing higher increases include Boston, Honolulu and Syracuse, while New Haven, CT, New Orleans, Cleveland, and Glendale, AZ, saw the largest drops  
While overall multifamily rents are still lower, the decline continues to decelerate (less bad), with some positive signs that normal seasonal patterns are returning.  

RETAIL/MALLS:
Class A malls defy retail gloom with luxury-fueled comeback (Real Deal): https://blinks.bloomberg.com/news/stories/TCD9EUC6YGW0 while the death of the mall was greatly exaggerated, the sector has largely become bifurcated:  
* Class A malls, which tend to be the dominant mall in their markets and in areas with stronger demographics/incomes, continue to see healthy occupancies and rental rate growth 
* An example cited is the Roosevelt Field mall on Long Island, which is owned by Simon Property Group, and has in line sales of around $1,250/sf 
* A decade ago, there were over 1,200 malls in the United States, however that number has fallen to around 900 today (I've seen lower numbers other places) 
* Of those 900, the top 100 malls represent around half of the entire sector's value 
* In contrast, the bottom 350 malls represent only 10% of the sector's value
* Revenues at top tier malls are running 5% higher annually, while lower tier malls are seeing a similar decline (meaning -5%)
* This is once again the K shaped recovery, with money going to the higher quality assets, while others remain challenged 
* The top mall operators, including Simon and Brookfield, continue to move away from the anchor driven model, emphasizing more luxury tenants, dining options, and experiential tenants  
* Brookfield's portfolio is around 95% occupied with tenant sales up 20% since 2019, with some trophy assets much higher
* The financing markets have warmed to the story, with CMBS issuance for malls doubling to $8bn in 2025  
* That being said, weaker malls have continued to struggle with about 40 malls closing annually, with 11% of malls tied to regional loans DQ per Trepp 
* Interestingly, the younger generation is returning to malls, as it enjoys the in-person experience
The death of the mall narrative was never fully grounded in reality, at least as it pertains to the higher quality malls. Higher quality operators/REITs got ahead of the trends in some properties, converting vacant anchor boxes to mixed uses including apartments, which can further benefit the mall component. While distress is likely to persist with lower quality assets, we have started seeing CMBS financings bleed over into what are likely considered B malls today.  

REIT REPORT: 
This week we look at the lodging sector via a REIT with a large portfolio in major markets and/or resort and convention markets across the U.S., Park Hotels & Resorts: 

Park Hotels & Resorts (ticker: PK): Park Hotels & Resorts Inc is a REIT which was spun off from Hilton Worldwide in 2017. It has a portfolio of 34 premium branded hotels with over 23,000-rooms, located in prime U.S. markets with high barriers to entry. Over 90% of the REITs portfolio is luxury or upper upscale properties, with 80% located in CBDs of major cities or resort or conference destinations. As such, they offer good insight into some of their markets, as well as having some CMBS exposure. 
* Park saw Q4 RevPAR up 0.8% on a YOY basis, but for FY 2025, RevPAR was down 2.0% 
* Core RevPAR was up 3.2% in Q4, however was still down 1.3% for FY 2025  
* Park intends to sell/divest its non-core assets as soon as commercially reasonable, as it wants to concentrate its focus on what it considers to be its 21 core hotels 
* Group performance was strong in Q4, supported by convention demand in Hawaii and New York, as well as solid group activity in Orlando 
* Hilton Hawaiian Village Waikiki Beach Resort (HILT 2016-HHV, CD 2017-CD3 and CD4, CFCRE 2016-C7, JPMCC 2016-JP4, JPMCC 2017-JP5, JPMDB 2017-C5, MSBAM 2016-C32, and WFCM 2016-C37) was one of its strongest performers, with Q4 RevPAR up 22% YOY, albeit comping to a very soft Q4 2024 
* PK has nearly completed the $75mm, multi-year guestroom renovation at the Rainbow Tower at HHV, as well as a renovation at its Waikoloa Village 
* The REIT will be launching a full-scale renovation at the Ali-I Tower at HHV impacting all 348 guestrooms, the tower lobby, the pool, and adding three additional keys - the total investment is expected to be $96mm
* Once this renovation is complete, around 80% of the rooms in Hilton Hawaiian Village will have been newly renovated 
* PK provided its plans to pay off the HHV CMBS loan - it plans to use some of the proceeds from a delayed draw term loan, as well as a planned financing on another asset (Bonnet Creek - not securitized) to fully repay the CMBS financing maturing in early November 
* PK expects a multi-year recovery towards prior peak levels in Hawaii, with early signs of the recovery beginning 
* The New York market, where it owns the New York Hilton Midtown, was also a strong performer with its highest Q4 group revenue in its history 
* Miami, where it owns the Royal Palm South Beach, remains one of the strongest hotel markets in the country, and PK is excited about the long-term outlook for this hotel that is closed for a complete renovation 
* PK sees some positives in 2026, including a relatively firm U.S. economy (this was pre-war), ongoing fiscal stimulus, a hopeful easing of inflation (again, pre-war), as well as a number of major events like the World Cup and America 250 celebrations 
* Additionally, supply growth remains at historical lows with construction muted, which should support healthy operating fundamentals over the next few years 
* That being said, concerns they noted include geopolitical and macroeconomic volatility creating uncertainty around booking decisions, as well as reduced international demand (particularly from Canada) 
* PK provided RevPAR guidance for 2026 of flat to up 2%, with expenses expected to be up low-single digits for the year
* The 2% to 3% growth in operating expenses is primarily driven by labor, which could be up 4% to 5% for the year 
* 2025 was a good year for insurance, with no major claims - so they aren't expecting an increase in premiums 
* The forecast includes low single digit growth at both Hawaiian hotels, driven by improving leisure transient demand post renovations
* Japanese visitation, which has traditionally been a significant driver for Hawaii, was relatively flat in 2025 
* PK does see some green shoots from Japan, expecting mid-single-digit growth in terms of visitation to around 750,000 visitors for 2026
* Forecasts suggest another 5% to 6% growth in Japanese visits for 2027 
* In terms of its dispositions, PK hasn't seen any issues for buyers in relation to credit, with significant availabilities of debt capital as well as private credit
* There is also significant availability of equity capital
* Buyers of its more non-core hotels have included family offices and owner-operators, including deep value entrepreneurs
* Some markets have been tougher than others for sales, including Chicago and Los Angeles, while San Francisco has been improving   
* PK referenced the K shaped recovery we continue to hear about, with the consumer remaining cautious
* Although not discussed on the call, PK sold the Hilton San Francisco Union Square and Parc 55 San Francisco (HILT 2016-SFP) in November 2025 for a combined $408mm (we'll briefly touch on news on the property under CMBS)   
Park spent some time discussing the Hawaii lodging market, and noted that while tourism from Japan remains lower/was relatively flat in 2025, there are signs it should improve in 2026 and 2027. The REIT remains bullish on the longer term outlook for Hawaii, and outlined its plans to pay off the HHV loan by its November 2026 maturity date.
