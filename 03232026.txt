Different cities, same story: Office tenants exit central business district for Century City and Uptown Dallas (Costar): https://www.costar.com/article/1498739340/different-cities-same-story-office-tenants-exit-central-business-district-for-century-city-and-uptown-dallas this was an interesting report, touching on trends in two office markets with CMBS exposure: 
* While every major city has its unique attributes/conditions, two downtown submarkets are facing similar trends - Dallas and Los Angeles 
* Both downtown submarkets have seen vacancies remain elevated as tenants opt for more attractive submarkets - in this case Uptown in Dallas and Century City in Los Angeles
* Downtown Los Angeles (DTLA) has struggled with decreasing foot traffic, amid growing concerns over homelessness and crime/safety
* Tenants have increasingly been attracted to Century City, which was the premiere location for office space even before the pandemic, with newer buildings and well maintained streets
* Despite increased activity in DTLA in 2025, net absorption remained negative at -122,236-sf  
* Conversely, Century City saw positive absorption of 161,250-sf over the same period 
* In 2024, DTLA saw -2.7mm sf of absorption, while Century City saw only -216,000-sf - the vacancy rates sit at 22.1% and 16.0%, respectively
* Even its significantly lower rates - averaging $37/sf - have not been enough to retain tenants 
* Tenants that have left downtown for Century City include Savills, Skadden Arps, and AEW Capital Management 
* Dallas faces similar dynamics, with downtown Dallas seeing vacancies around 500bps higher than Uptown Dallas (27% versus 22%), despite the submarkets being separated by only a two minute walk  
* Note that Uptown vacancies are due in part to some supply side pressures due to recent trophy completions 
* Numerous tenants are leaving downtown for the newer buildings/superior walkability of Uptown, including Bank of America, Deloitte and Invesco 
* Some downtown tenants are also leaving in favor of the suburbs, including AT&T's recent announcement of plans to relocate its HQ to Plano, TX - that is projected to push Downtown vacancies from 28% to around 33%
* The difference in asking rents between Downtown and Uptown has increased to $27/sf, which is up 72% from the four year average pre-COVID
There is also a good chart showing the occupancy shifts in both markets. There is CMBS exposure in both submarkets in each city.

REIT REPORT: 
This week we look at the latest on the life science sector via Alexandria Real Estate, and take a look at the more economy and mid-scale focused Choice Hotels: 

Alexandria Real Estate Equities (ticker: ARE): Alexandria is an S&P 500 urban office REIT, focusing on the life science real estate niche. The REIT develop, operates, and owns class A life science properties primarily clustered in key innovation markets like Boston, San Francisco, and NYC. It specializes in lab and office space leased to biotech, pharma, and technology tenants. As such, it offers a great read on the health of the life science space: 
* Alexandria noted that 2025 represented the fifth year of a life science bear market 
* The REIT had initially been positive on the potential of changes at the NIH and FDA, however numerous key departures at the agencies and some controversial changes have tempered that enthusiasm 
* ARE leased 1.2mm sf of GLA in Q4, which was up 14% over the same prior year period and 10% above the quarterly average over the prior two years 
* Occupancy ended 2025 at 90.9%, which was up 30bps sequentially - however, ARE reiterated its YE 2026 occupancy range of 87.7% to 89.3% (meaning it is heading lower)
* Occupancy will be impacted early in 2026 by 1.2mm sf of lease expirations, but should improve over the year as prospects have been identified for around 40% of the space
* Same property NOI was off 6% in Q4 and 3.5% for the year (-1.7% and +0.9% on a cash basis), with the mid-point for 2026 guidance at -8.5%, evidencing the ongoing pressure on the sector
* In addition to increasing dispositions, ARE is pivoting on several properties - opting to pursue office uses instead of life science 
* The REIT took $1.45 billion in impairments in Q4, and while a good amount of that was on land, some were on properties in the South San Francisco and South of Market submarkets
* Venture capital firms continue to be very conservative in deploying capital 
* With a lack of IPOs, LPs have been forced to stay in investments longer and haven't had capital to reinvest into other opportunities 
* While tours have been increasing, decision making continues to take longer as companies remain cautious around taking on new space or expanding
* While the XBI index (a biotech ETF) has outperformed over the past year, the majority of these companies are commercial or near commercial companies, which don't tend to drive lab space needs 
* There's been very little leasing for public biotech, which had been a mainstay of the industry
* In terms of its dispositions, they've primarily focused on non-core assets, with cap rates ranging from mid-6% to mid-9%
* The variance is largely due to the markets and the weighted average remaining lease term (WALT) - the lower the WALT, the higher the cap rate
* ARE is considering selling a few core properties which would provide price discovery for higher quality properties, with the REIT believing top tier assets should trade at a five handle cap rate
* Smaller spaces (under 50,000-sf) are seeing the most demand today, with the dearth of biotech/public biotech type companies weighing on the 50,000-sf to 150,000-sf segment 
* ARE noted the Greater Boston area did see an 11% increase in tenants in the market - the first increase in several quarters 
* ARE believes the FDA commissioner's focus on compressing the 10 to 12 year, billion dollar cycle of bringing a compound from discovery to the market is positive - however, the agency's been hurt by defections, DOGE firings, and resignations
* The NIH decision to limit indirect costs to 15% caused a big decline in demand and immediate decision making by a lot of medical institutions, however there was a court decision that overruled that, so we could see some movement there 
* Tenant improvements haven't changed, although they remain elevated for anything that is shell - ARE has been doing some pre-builts
* While TIs are stable, there continues to be pressure on free rent periods, for which it has been forced to meet the market to win deals
* ARE prefers longer free rent periods than reducing taking rents, as that is where you start to destruct value
* As long as availabilities remain in the mid-20s to low-30s in the major life science markets, free rent will remain pressured
* ARE is positive on the longer term outlook for the sector given only 10% of diseases have been addressed, however the REIT is a little distressed about the step back on some of the vaccine policies
* For the Greater Boston area, there is a significant amount of inventory in Somerville and other tertiary areas where it could take four to five years to resolve 
* Resolution in these submarkets that really didn't need to be life science markets will primarily be via change in use 
* In Cambridge, Watertown, and Seaport, where it is more focused, it could take two to three years and maybe even less to recover - these are functional lab markets that are dealing with oversupply 
* They expect a good number of owners to capitulate and go with a different use for their properties (ie away from life science), which would be positive/could reduce the recovery timeline 
Fundamentals in the life science sector clearly remain pressured as the market faces the fifth consecutive year in a bear market. While there was an increase in leasing activity, Alexandria expects both occupancy and NOI to decline further in 2026. It remains focused on dispositions of non-productive properties, as well as leasing. Given the softness in the market, it has been forced to offer higher concessions in terms of free rents, however it has been trying to hold the line on rents. It remains positive on its core submarkets longer term, as these are functional life science markets that need to work through excess supply, as opposed to some suburbs/other markets that really never had a reason to pursue life science opportunities.    

Choice Hotels International (NYSE: CHH): Choice Hotels is one of the world's largest franchisors, with over 7,400 hotels across 22 diverse brands ranging from economy to upscale. Some of its flags include Comfort Inn, Quality Inn, Cambria Hotels, and Radisson Americas. We wanted to get its read on the lodging sector given many of its brands are focused more on the economy and mid-scale segments.  
* Choice saw Q4 RevPAR in the U.S. fall 7.6%, with FY RevPAR off 3%
* The U.S. was impacted by a 540-bp hurricane benefit for the PY Q4, with RevPAR excluding that impact down 2.2% YOY (which was modest sequential improvement)  
* Q4 was also adversely impacted by the government shutdown as well as continued softness in international inbound travel, however it still achieved occupancy share index gains versus its competitors on a full year basis 
* Domestic RevPAR compares to +3.2% and +2.5% internationally for Q4 and FY, respectively 
* Overall, global RevPAR was -4.6% and -1.2%, respectively 
* CHH is expecting several more favorable tail winds in 2026, including tax refunds/stimulus, the weaker dollar leading to more domestic travel, and some major events including the Work Cup and the 250th Anniversary
* To date, tax refunds are running 11% higher YOY, while overall tax relief was up 18% - that has historically provided significant stimulus for travel within their segments  
* The sector is benefiting from a limited new supply backdrop as well as steady workforce based travel demand tied to infrastructure, manufacturing, and data center investment 
* Interestingly, CHH had also noted lower gas prices as a potential catalyst (the call was prior to the recent run up), for which it was expecting a pickup in road trips 
* They continue to see a value bias with their consumers, with a trade down still common 
* Choice is seeing an increase in conversions to its brands, with its conversion pipeline up 12% YOY 
* Conversions are positive, as they are 5x faster than new construction 
* Choice is increasingly constructive on U.S. lodging demand across its segments as the core customer continues to prioritize travel within their overall spending, with a clear focus on affordability 
* That should position Choice well, as they've long been strategically positioned at the center of value-driven travel
* Choice is very positive on the extended stay segment, where it saw its 10th consecutive quarter of double-digit system growth 
* Extended stay, which is 40% of its U.S. pipeline, benefits from longer average stays, higher margins for owners, and greater earnings stability across cycles
* It's largest under construction pipeline is for the economy and mid-scale extended stay segments, with a focus on strengthening its economy brands 
* Choice continues to exit underperforming hotels, with hotels exiting its system in 2025 having RevPAR 20% below the company average
* Franchisee retention in the U.S. remained strong, with renewal activity in 2025 matching prior all-time highs 
* CHH noted its occupancy index was positive in 2025, and based on prior cycles, occupancy is the first thing to recover
* Occupancy is typically followed by rate improvement, as once you are able to fill rooms, that provides more pricing power 
* There has been several years of almost no new construction across the industry in the U.S., with flattish RevPAR and higher interest rates making it much harder to finance new construction projects 
* New construction represents only around 15% to 20% of new room growth, versus around a third of additions historically 
* Choice sees a lot of its growth being international again in 2026, as it expects the general economic environment to be stronger in international markets 
Choice reported a decline in RevPAR for Q4 and FY 2025, although it does see some potential positives for 2026. Those include higher tax refunds, the weaker dollar likely keeping more people vacationing domestically, and a strong slate of major events that should be traffic drivers. The sector is also benefiting from several years of very limited new supply additions. It is still forecasting RevPAR growth in the U.S. of -2% to +1% for 2026, but expects conditions to be modestly better. It is focused on growing its extended stay brand, where it sees the fundamentals as strong.
